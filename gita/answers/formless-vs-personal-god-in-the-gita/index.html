Does the Gita favor a formless God or a personal God?

Chapter 12 opens with Arjuna putting the question to Krishna directly: between those steadfastly devoted to God's personal form and those who worship the formless, unmanifest Brahman, which do you consider more perfect in yoga (12.1)? Krishna's answer is not a flat ranking so much as a practical judgment about human capacity. He states that those who fix their minds on him with unwavering faith he considers most perfect in yoga (12.2), and he does not deny that those devoted to the unmanifest, imperishable, all-pervading, inconceivable, and immovable Absolute also reach him (12.3–12.4) — but he adds a caveat that shapes the rest of the chapter: the path of the unmanifest is exceedingly troublesome for embodied beings, because a mind still identified with a body struggles to fix itself on something without attributes, name, or form (12.5). Devotion to a personal, describable form gives the mind something concrete to hold onto, which is why Krishna calls it the easier and, for most practitioners, the wiser route — not because the formless Absolute is a lesser reality, but because most human minds are not yet equipped to sustain contemplation of it. The rest of chapter 12 (12.6–12.12) then lays out a graded ladder for those who can't yet manage full devotion: fix the mind on God; if that's too hard, practice the discipline of remembrance; if that's too hard, work for God's sake; and if even that's too hard, renounce the fruits of action instead. The formless-versus-personal question, in other words, resolves less into a metaphysical verdict and more into a matter of meeting the practitioner where they actually are.
Source: Bhagavad Gita 12.1–12.12
