What does the Bhagavad Gita say about courage and fearlessness?

In chapter 16, Krishna gives Arjuna a list of twenty-six qualities that make up what he calls the “divine nature” (daivi sampad) — the traits that lead toward liberation, contrasted with a shorter list of qualities that lead toward bondage. The list doesn't open with compassion or non-violence, the qualities readers might expect first; it opens with abhayam — fearlessness (16.1–3). Traditional commentary treats this ordering as deliberate: fear, more than any other single obstacle, is what keeps a person from acting rightly, believing sincerely, or giving generously, so it has to be addressed before the qualities built on top of it can take hold. The Gita's fearlessness isn't recklessness or the absence of ordinary caution; it's specifically freedom from anxiety about outcomes a person can't control — loss, failure, death, the future in general. That connects directly to the book's central argument in chapter 2, where Krishna grounds Arjuna's courage on the battlefield in the claim that the true self cannot actually be destroyed by any weapon (2.23). Commentators read this as the source of the fearlessness listed later in chapter 16: it isn't manufactured through willpower or bravado, it follows from a settled conviction about what a person actually is. The text is explicit that this quality has to be paired with the others on the same list — purity of mind, charity, self-control, truthfulness — because fearlessness alone, without those, curdles into aggression rather than courage. Krishna's own life becomes the illustration Arjuna is meant to draw from: he needs to act, not to feel safe first, and the text treats fearlessness as what makes that order of operations possible.
Source: Bhagavad Gita 16.1–3; 2.23
