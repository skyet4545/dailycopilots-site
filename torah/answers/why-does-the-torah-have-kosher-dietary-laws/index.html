Why does the Torah have kosher dietary laws?

The Torah's dietary laws (kashrut) are set out mainly in Leviticus 11 and Deuteronomy 14 — which animals may be eaten (land animals that both chew the cud and have split hooves, fish with fins and scales, and so on) and which may not. But the Torah's own stated reason is not health and not hygiene; it is holiness. Leviticus 11 closes with God's rationale: “For I am the LORD your God; sanctify yourselves therefore, and be holy, for I am holy… For I am the LORD who brought you up out of the land of Egypt, to be your God; you shall therefore be holy, for I am holy” (Leviticus 11:44–45). Eating — the most ordinary and animal of human acts — is turned into a daily discipline of set-apartness. Classically, the dietary laws are treated as chukim: statutes whose full reasoning is not spelled out, kept as acts of obedience and sanctification rather than because their logic is self-evident. Later commentators offered explanations (self-discipline, compassion for animals, separateness from surrounding nations), and folk tradition added health rationales, but the text itself grounds kashrut in imitating God's holiness, not in avoiding disease. That is why observance persists even where the practical explanations don't apply: the aim is to make the ordinary table a place of sanctity. How each category and its boundary cases work in practice is where the study deepens.
Source: Leviticus 11:44–45
