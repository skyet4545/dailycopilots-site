What does the Torah say about honoring your parents?

The command to honor parents is the fifth of the Ten Commandments, given twice in nearly identical language: “Honor your father and your mother, that your days may be long upon the land which the LORD your God is giving you” (Exodus 20:12), repeated with an added phrase in Deuteronomy 5:16 — “that it may go well with you.” It's the only one of the Ten Commandments paired with an explicit reward, which rabbinic tradition reads as a signal of how seriously the Torah takes it, not as a literal trade of longevity for compliance. The Hebrew for honor, kibbud, comes from kavod — weight, significance — and the Talmud (Kiddushin 31b) spells out what that weight looks like in practice: feeding a parent, clothing them, helping them come and go, and not contradicting or interrupting them. Jewish law distinguishes kibbud (honor, active care) from a related duty, mora (reverence) — not sitting in a parent's designated seat, not speaking in their place. What makes the commandment theologically unusual is its placement: it sits on the first tablet, traditionally understood to contain duties toward God, rather than the second tablet's duties toward other people. The Talmud explains why with a striking equation: since a person has three partners in their existence — God, father, and mother — honoring the two human partners is treated as inseparable from honoring the divine one. That doesn't make honoring parents unconditional; classical sources are clear it doesn't extend to obeying a parent's instruction to violate Torah law. But it does mean the Torah frames this specific relationship as the place where a person first practices the kind of respect owed to God.
Source: Exodus 20:12; Deuteronomy 5:16; Talmud Kiddushin 31b
