Is desire the root of suffering? What different traditions say

Two traditions that never met reached almost the same conclusion: suffering grows less from what happens to us than from our craving for things to be otherwise. Buddhism states it as the Second Noble Truth — that the origin of suffering (dukkha) is taṇhā, literally “thirst”: craving for sensual pleasure, for becoming, and for non-becoming. The problem is not that we have preferences but that we cling to impermanent things as if they could be held forever. Stoicism arrives independently at a close parallel. Epictetus calls the wrongly aimed appetite orexis — desire directed at things outside our control, which leaves us elated when we get them and devastated when we don't. His prescription in the Enchiridion is to confine desire to “what is up to us” (our judgments, choices, and character) and to withhold it from “what is not up to us” (health, reputation, outcomes). Crucially, neither tradition says “want nothing.” The Buddha affirmed chanda, desire aimed at the path itself; the Stoics affirmed desire aimed at virtue. Each distinguishes grasping craving from healthy aspiration. What they share is the diagnosis: suffering comes from demanding that the uncontrollable bend to you, and easing that grip — not rearranging the world — is where peace begins. Seeing how Buddhist, Stoic, and other traditions map the same terrain in their own vocabulary is where the practice gets specific.
Source: The Second Noble Truth (Pali Canon); Epictetus, Enchiridion 1–2
